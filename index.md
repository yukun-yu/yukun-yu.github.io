## About Me

As a Ph.D. student in Developmental Psychology at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, I work under the guidance of Dr. Cynthia Fisher. My research focuses on how children navigate linguistic ambiguities to infer intended meanings. Specifically, I investigate children's adaptation to recent experiences in resolving syntactic ambiguities and explore how syntactic knowledge guides sentence parsing and word learning. My research methodology incorporates eye-tracking methods to study language comprehension and word acquisition in infants and preschoolers.

Beyond academia, I find joy in capturing fleeting moments with instant cameras. The unpredictability of film fascinates me, as it encapsulates not just the image but also the ambient conditions—temperature, humidity, and lighting—of each snapshot. My love for the outdoors stems from cherished childhood memories of hiking with family and friends, particularly near my grandparents' home in the mountainous suburbs of Beijing. The fall season there holds a special place in my heart: gathering fallen chestnuts, breathing in the crisp air, and perching atop trees to look out over the cornfields in the village below.

## Research Interests

- **Language Acquisition:** word learning, syntactic bootstrapping, early acquisition of semantic-syntax links
- **Syntactic Adaptation:** syntactic ambiguity resolution, syntactic priming, verb bias adaptation
- **Increamental Language Processing:** online language comprehension, prediction, error-based implicit learning
